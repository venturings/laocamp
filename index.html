Chapter 2 · History & Foundations

📜 Where Laos Comes From

More than 650 years of kings, golden ages, invasions and revolutions — understanding the past explains why Laos is the way it is today.

🥥 The Origin Story & Founding Myth▾

Long ago — Lao legend says after the year 877 — a hero named Khun Bôrom came down to earth on a giant vine during a time of chaos. He had seven sons, and he sent them out to found the kingdoms of the region. His eldest son, Khun Lo, made his capital at Muang Sua (the city later called Luang Prabang), becoming the first ruler in the Lao royal line.

This story was written down around 1503 in a famous text called the Nithān Khun Bôrom. For Lao people, Khun Bôrom is honored as the father of the nation, and every later king claimed to descend from him. The first peoples of the land, though, were the indigenous groups (today's Lao Theung) who lived there long before the Tai-Lao arrived from the north.

Sources: Stuart-Fox, A History of Laos (1997); Contemporary Laos (1983).

🐘 The Golden Age: the Kingdom of Lan Xang▾

In 1353, a warrior-prince named Fa Ngum — who had grown up at the powerful Khmer court of Angkor and married a Khmer princess — returned with an army and united the scattered Lao towns (muang) into one great kingdom: LAN XANG HOM KHAO, "The Kingdom of a Million Elephants and the White Parasol." The name boasted of military power; the white parasol was a symbol of kingship.

Lan Xang lasted about three and a half centuries. It wasn't ruled like a modern country with fixed borders — it was a mandala, a network where local lords stayed loyal, paid tribute, and sent soldiers when called. Its wealth came from trade in forest treasures like ivory, benzoin (a fragrant resin) and the sticklac used to make lacquerware, plus silk, salt and iron.

The great kings

King Visun (1500–1520) brought the sacred Phra Bang Buddha image to the capital and had the legends written down. King Setthathirat (1500s) defied the Burmese invaders, built the golden That Luang stupa, and moved the capital to Vientiane in 1560. King Souligna Vongsa (1600s) ruled over the last great era of peace and learning — Vientiane became famous as a center of Buddhist study, attracting monks from across Asia.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Stuart-Fox (1997).

⚔️ Conflicts & Traumas: invasions, a split & a tragic king▾

The kingdom breaks apart (1690s–1707)

When King Souligna Vongsa died in the 1690s with no clear heir, wars over the throne tore Lan Xang into three smaller kingdoms: Luang Prabang in the north, Vientiane in the center, and Champasak in the south. Divided, they grew weak and fell under the control of powerful Siam (today's Thailand).

King Anouvong's rebellion (1827)

The Vientiane king Anouvong (Chao Anou) tried to win back Lao freedom from Siam. His armies marched south — but his plan failed, the Siamese counter-attacked, and in 1828 they completely destroyed Vientiane, deporting much of its people. Anouvong was captured and died a prisoner. Today he is honored as a national hero who dared to fight for independence; there's a big statue of him on the Vientiane riverfront.

The Secret War (1964–1973)

The deepest scar came in the 20th century. During the Vietnam War era, Laos became the most heavily bombed country in history. You'll find the full, careful story in the "Laos Today" chapter.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Stuart-Fox (1997).

🇫🇷 Colonization: the French era (1893–1953)▾

In 1887, after the city of Luang Prabang was raided, its king Oun Kham asked France for protection. Through the Franco-Siamese Treaty of 1893, Laos became a French protectorate — part of "French Indochina" with Vietnam and Cambodia. France ruled for about 50 years.

The French mostly left the king on his throne as a figurehead and ran things through a small, French-educated elite. They built some roads and towns and introduced things still seen today — like the baguette and the ball game pétanque — but they also taxed people heavily, which sparked rebellions (such as the Bolaven revolt led by Ong Kaeo and Kommadam, and the Hmong revolt led by Ba Chay).

During World War II, Japanese forces took over Laos (1945). When the war ended, a powerful movement for independence had been born.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Stuart-Fox (1997).

🕊️ Independence & Revolution (1945–1975)▾

After WWII, Lao nationalists formed the Lao Issara ("Free Lao") movement. Full independence from France came in 1953–54. But the country was immediately split between rival sides during the Cold War — famously led by "the three princes": the neutralist Souvanna Phouma, the rightist Boun Oum, and the communist "Red Prince" Souphanouvong, who led the Pathet Lao.

A long civil war (the "thirty-year struggle") followed, tangled up with the Vietnam War next door. In 1975, the Pathet Lao won. The 600-year-old monarchy ended, and the Lao People's Democratic Republic was founded as a one-party socialist state, led by Kaysone Phomvihane — still honored on banknotes and statues today.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Stuart-Fox (1997).

Chapter 3 · Society & The People

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 The People of Laos

About 7.8 million people from roughly 50 ethnic groups — making Laos one of the most diverse countries in Southeast Asia for its size.

🏔️ Three Great Groups — by where they live

Laos has long grouped its many cultures by elevation — a simple, friendly way to picture the country. Each group includes many distinct peoples with their own languages, clothes, and customs.

🌾

Lao Loum

"Lowland Lao"

The majority. They live in river valleys, grow rice in paddies, and gave Laos its national language and Buddhist culture. Includes the Phuan and Tai Dam.

⛰️

Lao Theung

"Mid-slope Lao"

Among the oldest inhabitants of the land, like the Khmu and Lamet. They live on hillsides and were here long before the Tai-Lao arrived.

🏔️

Lao Soung

"Mountain-top Lao"

The Hmong, Yao (Mien) and others who came from southern China in the 1800s and farm the high ridges. Famous for colorful embroidery and silver jewelry.

Sources: Contemporary Laos (1983); Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992).

🤝 Values: "We" Before "Me"

Laos is a deeply collectivist culture: family, village and community come before the individual. People prize being calm, polite, generous and easygoing — losing your temper in public is seen as embarrassing.

"Success" isn't only about money. It's about having a good reputation, caring for your parents, making merit, and enjoying life. There's a famous attitude called "muan" (fun) — if there's no joy in something, why bother? — and the gentle phrase ບໍ່ເປັນຫຍັງ (baw pen nyang), "never mind, it's fine."

🙏 Religion & Belief Systems

Most Lao are Theravada Buddhists. The big idea is karma and merit: do good deeds now for a better life next time. Giving food to monks each morning is the most cherished way to make merit.

Older animist beliefs blend right in. Many Lao believe in phi (spirits) that live in rivers, trees and houses, and people build little spirit houses for them. The much-loved baci ceremony — tying white strings around your wrists for good luck and to keep your spirit strong — comes from these pre-Buddhist beliefs. There are also smaller communities of Christians and Muslims.

🛕 Deep dive: monks, the wat & the Sangha▾

The wat (temple) is the heart of village life — not just for worship, but as a school, a meeting hall, and a community center. The community of monks is called the Sangha.

Many Lao boys become a novice monk for a while — sometimes just for a few weeks, sometimes for years. It's considered a great way to make merit for your family and, historically, one of the only ways a poor village boy could get an education. Monks wake before dawn, walk the streets collecting food offerings (alms), study scriptures, and help at festivals.

Within a temple you'll see a that (a tower-shaped monument built over a holy relic), a sala (open pavilion where people gather), and the main hall where the Buddha images are kept.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Contemporary Laos (1983); Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (2021).

Going Deeper🏔️ The Hmong story: a people across borders▾

To understand modern Laos, you have to understand the Hmong — a mountain people whose story stretches from southern China to the suburbs of Minneapolis and Sydney.

Who they are

The Hmong moved into northern Laos from southern China during the 1800s, settling the highest ridges. Their society is organized into clans, with strong traditions of language, embroidery, silver jewelry, and the haunting reed-pipe music of the qeej. Across Laos, China, Vietnam and Thailand, there are millions of Hmong; in Laos itself, they are one of the largest highland minorities.

The Secret War years (1960s–1975)

During the Vietnam War, the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency recruited a "Secret Army" in the Lao mountains, mostly Hmong, led by General Vang Pao. The army fought communist Pathet Lao and North Vietnamese forces — and paid an enormous price. Historians estimate 30,000 Hmong died in the war, more than 10% of their entire population in Laos. Many were promised a homeland of their own if the side they fought for won.

When the U.S. withdrew in 1975 and the communist Pathet Lao took power, thousands of Hmong fled — some airlifted by CIA aircraft to Thai refugee camps, many more walking out on foot. On 29 May 1975 at Hin Heup bridge, Pathet Lao guards opened fire on a refugee column, killing dozens.

The diaspora and today

From the refugee camps, hundreds of thousands resettled in the United States (especially Minnesota, Wisconsin, and California), France, and Australia. Vang Pao himself died in exile in California in 2011. The Hmong-American author Kao Kalia Yang and rapper-actress Sunisa Lee (Olympic gymnastics gold medalist) are part of this generation.

For Hmong who stayed, the postwar years were hard: relocation away from traditional highland farms, suspicion of disloyalty, restrictions on opium cultivation that had been their cash crop. Poverty among rural Hmong remains higher than the national average. A small Hmong armed resistance held out in remote mountains for decades after 1975, mostly fading by the 2000s. Today, Hmong serve in government at many levels — but anthropologists who work with the community note that a sense of marginalization persists.

"The Hmong generally have a clear sense of identity and distinction from other ethnic groups… In Thailand and Laos, Hmong argue that they are different from lowland Lao." — Phill Wilcox, 2021

Sources: Wilcox, Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (2021); Stuart-Fox, Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Contemporary Laos (1983); Baird (2010); Vang (2011); Yang, The Latehomecomer (2008).

🚦 Unwritten Rules of Politeness

Lao manners are gentle and important. Here's how to be a respectful guest:

Do

  • Greet with a "nop" — palms pressed together, a slight bow.
  • Take your shoes off before entering a home or temple.
  • Dress modestly at temples (cover shoulders and knees).
  • Use your right hand, or both hands, to give and receive.
  • Lower your head a little when passing in front of older people.
  • Smile — it's the national superpower!

Don't

  • Touch anyone's head — it's the most respected part of the body.
  • Point your feet at people or at a Buddha image.
  • Lose your temper or raise your voice in public.
  • Touch a monk if you are a girl or woman.
  • Step over food or over someone sitting on the floor.
Chapter 4 · Culture & Creative Expression

🎨 How Lao People Express Themselves

Food, music, dance, stories and crafts — the joyful, creative soul of Laos.

🍚 Cuisine & Food Culture

The heart of every Lao meal is sticky rice (ເຂົ້າໜຽວ, khao niaw), eaten by rolling it into a little ball with your right hand and dipping it into shared dishes. Lao food is famously fresh, herby, sour and spicy, with lots of lemongrass, lime, chili, mint and a fermented fish seasoning called padaek.

Meals are communal and relaxed: dishes go in the middle and everyone shares, often sitting on a mat on the floor. Eating together is a big part of being family and friends.

🥤 Did you know? Beerlao is the country's famous national beer — and Lao coffee, grown on the cool Bolaven Plateau, is loved around the world.

💬 Language, Slang & Sayings▾

Lao communication is usually indirect and gentle. People rarely give a blunt "no" — they'll soften it or hint instead, to keep everyone comfortable. Staying pleasant matters more than being perfectly direct.

Famous everyday expressions: "baw pen nyang" (never mind / it's all good), and the idea of "muan" (fun) — Lao people genuinely value enjoying life. Because Lao and Thai are cousins, Lao speakers can often understand a lot of Thai TV and music, too.

📚 Arts, Literature & Folklore▾

Stories & epics

The most beloved Lao epic is Sinxay (Sinsay) — a long adventure poem about a heroic prince on a quest, written by the poet Pang Kham. Lao people also love their own version of the great Indian Ramayana, called Phra Lak Phra Lam, performed in dance and shadow plays. And the Nithān Khun Bôrom tells the nation's origin legend.

Crafts

Laos is famous for gorgeous handwoven silk textiles — each region has its own patterns, and the sinh (a wrap skirt) is the traditional dress for women. Other treasures: silverwork, lacquerware, and pottery, made in villages of skilled artisans for centuries.

Architecture

Lao temples have graceful, low, layered roofs that sweep toward the ground. The naga — a mythical river serpent — guards stairways and rooftops everywhere. The most famous buildings are the golden That Luang in Vientiane (the national symbol), the dazzling Wat Xieng Thong in Luang Prabang, and the ancient Khmer ruin of Vat Phou in the south.

Sources: Historical Dictionary of Laos (1992); Stuart-Fox (1997).

🎶 Music & Dance — Old & New

Traditional

The signature sound of Laos is the khaen — a bamboo mouth-organ whose music UNESCO recognizes as part of humanity's heritage. Mor lam is the lively, witty folk-singing style that goes with it. At nearly every celebration people do the graceful lamvong, a gentle circle dance where you move your hands in slow, beautiful shapes.

Going Deeper🛕 When tradition meets tourism: Luang Prabang & UNESCO▾

If you go to Luang Prabang at dawn, you'll see the famous tak bat: lines of orange-robed monks walking silently through the streets to receive food from kneeling locals. It is a 600-year-old ritual. It is also one of the most photographed scenes in Southeast Asia.

In 1995, UNESCO listed Luang Prabang as a World Heritage Site. That label brought money, fame, and a tidal wave of tourists — far more than the small town had ever seen. It saved old French-colonial houses and golden temples from being torn down. It also changed everyday life in ways nobody fully predicted.

What the locals notice

Phill Wilcox, an anthropologist who lived in Luang Prabang for fieldwork, recorded what residents quietly told her:

  • The morning alms-giving is now sometimes "performed" for tourists who push too close, use flash photography, or stand over monks while offering food (a major breach of etiquette).
  • Some monks are very young novices from poor families — and tourist behaviour can be confusing or upsetting for them.
  • Building rules meant to preserve the look of the town are sometimes ignored, even by people who criticize others for ignoring them.
  • Rents and house prices have risen, pushing some Lao families out of the old town.
  • Many people genuinely believe UNESCO listing has been good for the town and for Laos's image — even when they have private reservations.
"The Lao people just do not know how to look after this town. UNESCO is the best thing to have ever happened here!" — overheard from an expat guesthouse manager who, in private, ignored UNESCO building rules himself. (Wilcox, 2021)

The bigger question

Heritage tourism asks an awkward question: who is a place really for — the people who live there, or the outsiders who want to see it preserved? Luang Prabang isn't the only place facing this. Venice, Kyoto, Machu Picchu, and dozens of other heritage cities are wrestling with the same tension. Laos is going through it in fast-forward, against the backdrop of new wealth, Chinese investment, and the high-speed railway that now drops thousands more visitors at the station every day.

Source: Wilcox, Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (Amsterdam University Press, 2021); UNESCO World Heritage Centre.

Chapter 5 · Daily Life & Rhythms

📅 A Year & A Day in Laos

From water-fight New Year festivals to rocket launches and weekend takraw games, here is the rhythm of Lao life.

🎉 The Festival Calendar (called "Boun")

Almost every month of the Lao lunar calendar has a festival. They're called boun (which means "merit") and usually take place at a temple. Some come from Buddhism, others from older harvest and rain rituals — now happily mixed together. Tap a highlighted month!

💦 Spotlight: Pii Mai — Lao New Year▾

In mid-April, the whole country celebrates Pii Mai for about three days — the biggest party of the year. People clean their homes and Buddha statues, visit temples, build little sand stupas by the river, and ask elders for blessings.

And then… the water fights! Everyone splashes everyone — buckets, hoses, water guns — to wash away the old year and bring good luck (and to cool off, because April is the hottest month). If you visit during Pii Mai, expect to get completely soaked, and to love it.

⚽ Sports & Free Time

The most-loved local game is kataw (sepak takraw): like volleyball, but you may only use your feet, knees, head and chest to send a woven rattan ball over the net — the acrobatic kicks are amazing to watch. Football (soccer) is hugely popular, and you'll see older folks playing pétanque (a French ball game) in town squares.

Evenings and weekends mean hanging out by the Mekong at sunset, browsing buzzing night markets, sharing food, and — during festival season — cheering at thrilling longboat races.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family Life

Families are large and close. Grandparents, parents, aunts and uncles often live together or nearby, and older relatives are cared for at home by the family — there's a strong duty to look after your parents when they age.

Children are expected to respect and obey their elders. Cousins grow up almost like brothers and sisters. Many boys spend time as a novice monk, which is both a blessing for the family and a route to schooling.

Extended familyCare for eldersRespect

🏫 School & Work Culture

Children usually start primary school around age 6. Since 1975, Laos has worked hard to reduce illiteracy and now treats education as a top national priority — though in remote mountain villages, schools can still be far away and resources limited, and not every child finishes secondary school.

Attitudes toward teachers, elders and bosses are respectful and fairly formal. Work life is generally more relaxed and less rushed than in giant neighboring cities, and there is real value placed on not being in a hurry and keeping a peaceful mood.

Source: Contemporary Laos (1983); Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (2021).

🗺️ Landscape

Laos is the only landlocked country in Southeast Asia — no sea coast at all! Five neighbors wrap around it: China and Myanmar to the north, Vietnam to the east (along the long Annamite mountain range), Cambodia to the south, and Thailand to the west (along the Mekong).

About 70% of the country is rugged mountains and forest, so people settle in the valleys and along rivers. The highest peak is Phou Bia (2,820 m). There are three big upland plateaus: the Xieng Khouang Plateau (home of the Plain of Jars) in the north, the Nakai/Khammouane Plateau in the center, and the cool, fertile Bolaven Plateau in the south (famous for coffee).

🌊 The Mighty Mekong & Climate

The Mekong River is the lifeline of Laos — it forms most of the western border with Thailand and gives water, fish, transport and electricity. Rapids and the huge Khone Falls in the south divide it into regions, each with its own character.

The climate is tropical with two seasons: a hot, dry season (roughly November–April) and a rainy monsoon season (May–October), when warm winds bring the rains that feed the rice fields.

💰 How the Economy Works

Most families are farmers, especially of rice. The big modern money-makers are:

⚡

Hydropower

Dams on the rivers make electricity that Laos sells to its neighbors — it calls itself "the Battery of Southeast Asia."

⛏️

Mining

Gold, copper and other minerals are dug from the mountains.

🧳

Tourism

Visitors come for the temples, mountains, rivers and the famously relaxed pace of life.

The money is the Lao kip (₭). Laos is still one of the region's poorer, "least-developed" countries, and in recent years has struggled with rising prices (inflation) and large debts. Closing the gap between rich and poor — and between city and countryside — is a major goal.

🚄 The Laos–China Railway

In 2021, a high-speed railway opened, linking the capital Vientiane all the way to China. For a country that was once one of the hardest places to travel through, this was huge — fast trains now carry tourists and cargo and plug Laos into the rest of Asia. But it cost a fortune to build, adding to the country's debts, and some Lao people worry about how much influence China is gaining. It's one of the biggest stories of modern Laos.

🏙️ City vs. Village

Life looks very different in Vientiane — a lively riverside capital with cafés, scooters, shopping malls and the railway — compared with remote mountain villages, where families farm, may have little electricity or internet, and travel by boat or dirt road. Bridging this urban–rural divide with schools, roads and jobs is one of the country's biggest challenges. Many young people move to towns, or to Thailand, looking for work.

Going Deeper🌊 "The Battery of Southeast Asia" — the Mekong dam dilemma▾

Laos has a long-running national strategy: turn its rivers into electricity, sell it to Thailand, Vietnam, China and Cambodia, and earn the foreign currency the country desperately needs. Hydropower already accounts for around 30% of all exports, and the government plans to build more — well over 100 dams in total when smaller projects are counted. The slogan: "the Battery of Southeast Asia."

Why it's attractive

Unlike coal, hydropower is low-carbon. Unlike importing oil, it earns money rather than spends it. For a small, poor, landlocked country with steep rivers, it's an obvious resource to tap. Dam revenues fund the budget — schools, roads, debt payments.

The other side

Dams change rivers in ways that don't show up in a balance sheet:

  • Fish migrations — the Mekong is one of the world's most productive inland fisheries, feeding tens of millions across four countries. Dam walls block the long migrations many species need. Catches downstream in Cambodia and Vietnam have already dropped.
  • Sediment — the Mekong carries fertile silt that builds and rebuilds Vietnam's vital Mekong Delta. Dams trap that silt. The delta is now sinking.
  • Local displacement — every major dam means relocating villages. Compensation has often been criticized as inadequate.
  • Safety — in July 2018, the Xepian-Xe Nam Noy saddle dam collapsed during heavy rain, releasing a wall of water that killed dozens of people, destroyed villages, and displaced thousands. It became a national reckoning about whether dam-building was being done safely or rushed for export profits.

The hard question

Laos is one of the world's poorest countries and has every right to develop. Telling it not to use its rivers — when rich countries built dams for a century — sounds unfair. But the Mekong is shared. A decision in Vientiane affects a fisher in Cambodia and a rice farmer in Vietnam. Who decides? And how do you value a fish migration that has happened for thousands of years against the kilowatt-hours that pay this year's hospital bills? There's no clean answer. It is, exactly, a dilemma.

Sources: Mekong River Commission; International Rivers; The Economist; Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (2021).

Chapter 7 · Laos Today

🏛️ Modern Politics & Current Identity

The forces shaping the country right now, in 2026 — from the railway to China, from the kip's slide to the next generation's choices.

🗳️ How Laos Is Governed

Laos is a one-party socialist republic. There is only one legal political party: the Lao People's Revolutionary Party (LPRP), which the constitution calls the "leading nucleus" of the country. People vote for a National Assembly every five years, and those members then choose the country's top leaders — rather than the public electing them directly. The most recent election was in February 2026.

Going Deeper🐉 The China question: partner, or new patron?▾

No single issue dominates conversation about Laos's future more than its relationship with China. For decades Laos leaned politically on Vietnam, its socialist "elder brother." Since the 2010s, China has overtaken everyone else as the biggest investor, lender, and infrastructure builder in the country. The Laos–China Railway, opened in 2021, is the most visible symbol: a $6 billion high-speed line linking Kunming to Vientiane, part of China's Belt and Road Initiative. From Vientiane you can now reach Beijing in about 30 hours — something unimaginable a generation ago.

But the railway also accounts for a large slice of public debt. Independent analysts warn that Laos's debt load — much of it owed to Chinese banks — has reached levels that pressure the currency (the kip) and limit what the government can spend on schools and hospitals. By 2023, the kip had lost roughly half its value against the U.S. dollar.

What Lao people actually say

Anthropologist Phill Wilcox spent years interviewing people in Luang Prabang about this. She found a striking pattern: in public, citizens repeated the official line that the railway is good for development. In private, many worried that "Lao people will be working for the Chinese" and that "Laos will become increasingly Chinese." Even land sales to Chinese investors near the new railway stations have become a quiet but real source of resentment.

"Chinese influence, Chinese presence, or simply the issue of China in Laos, is the most important issue facing the country." — repeated theme from Wilcox's interviewees, 2018–2020

Two ways to read it

The optimistic view: the railway breaks Laos's curse of being landlocked, slashes transport costs for coffee, electricity, and tourism, and brings the country into a real regional economy. Without Chinese capital, none of it would have happened.

The skeptical view: the deal swaps geographic landlockedness for financial landlockedness — debt that gives Beijing leverage over Lao foreign policy, and a pattern that mirrors what Lao leaders once said about French colonialism.

Sources: Wilcox, Heritage and the Making of Political Legitimacy in Laos (Amsterdam University Press, 2021); Morris, Center for Global Development (2019); Radio Free Asia; Laotian Times.

Going Deeper💣 The "Secret War" in numbers — and why it isn't over▾

Between 1964 and 1973, the United States flew 580,000+ bombing sorties over Laos — one planeload every 8 minutes for nine years — without ever declaring war on the country. The official aim was to cut the Ho Chi Minh Trail and prop up the anti-communist Royal Lao government. The total dropped: over 2 million tons of ordnance — more than was used in the whole of WWII in Europe and the Pacific combined, on a country smaller than Britain.

The human toll

Historians estimate 200,000+ Lao dead and roughly twice that many wounded during the war. Around 750,000 people — a quarter of the population — were displaced. An estimated 30,000 Hmong died fighting in the CIA-backed "Secret Army" alone, more than 10% of their entire population in Laos.

The bombs that didn't explode

Roughly 30% of bombs failed to detonate on impact — leaving an estimated 80 million unexploded cluster bomblets ("UXO") still buried in Lao soil today. They still kill and maim people every year — a disproportionate number of them children who pick up the round metal "bombies" thinking they're toys. Clearance organizations like MAG, HALO, and the government's UXO Lao work province by province; in 2008 Laos joined the Convention on Cluster Munitions and hosted its first meeting. At current rates of clearance, parts of the country won't be safe for another century.

Why this still shapes the country

UXO contamination keeps farmland unusable in many provinces — a hidden brake on rural development. Tourists visit COPE in Vientiane to see prosthetic legs made for survivors. The U.S. did not formally acknowledge the bombing program until decades later and has since funded clearance, but at a level critics call modest compared to what was dropped. For Laos, the "Secret War" is not history — it's something you still have to walk carefully across.

Sources: Stuart-Fox, A History of Laos (Cambridge, 1997); Legacies of War; UXO Lao; MAG International; Convention on Cluster Munitions.

🔥 Current Flashpoints

💵 Economy & debt

The kip has lost roughly half its value against the dollar since 2022, food and fuel prices have climbed sharply, and government debt — much of it owed to China — sits at levels economists call "in distress." This is the single biggest worry in everyday conversation.

🌳 Dams on the Mekong

Laos hopes to be the "battery of Southeast Asia," exporting hydropower. But the same dams change fish migrations and sediment flow, hurting farmers and fishers in Cambodia and Vietnam downstream. The 2018 collapse of the Xepian-Xe Nam Noy dam — which killed dozens and displaced thousands — made this a national debate.

📱 Young people leaving

An estimated 200,000+ Lao work in Thailand at any given time. For a country of 7.8 million, this is a major brain drain — and at the same time, a vital source of family remittances.

🌏 Friends & Neighbors

Laos keeps an especially close "special relationship" with Vietnam, dating from the war years. It has grown very close to China, its biggest investor and the railway partner — though, as researchers found, some Lao people feel uneasy about how much land and business Chinese investors now control.

With Thailand, Laos shares deep family, cultural and language links (the two languages are cousins). Laos is a proud member of ASEAN, the club of ten Southeast Asian nations, and has even taken its turn hosting the big ASEAN summit.

💣 A Scar From the Past: The "Secret War"

Between 1964 and 1973, during the Vietnam War era, Laos became the most heavily bombed country in history — over 2 million tons of bombs, roughly a planeload every 8 minutes. The U.S. did this secretly to disrupt enemy supply routes (the "Ho Chi Minh Trail") that ran through Laos, and to support anti-communist forces. About 30% of the bombs never exploded.

Today around 80 million of these "UXO" (unexploded ordnance) still lie hidden across all of Laos's provinces, and they still injure people — many of them children — every year. Brave clearance teams (including all-female teams!) and groups like MAG and COPE work to make the land safe again. It's a hard story — but Lao people rebuild with remarkable strength, even turning old bomb metal into spoons, bells and jewelry.

Sources: HISTORY.com; Legacies of War; MAG. (A serious topic — if it makes you sad, that's okay. It matters.)

Chapter 12 · Discussion

🤔 Big Questions About Laos's Future

No right answers here — just hard, interesting questions worth thinking about, debating with friends, or writing about in class. Each one has a "case for" and a "case against" to get you started.

🇨🇳 1. Should Laos take Chinese investment if it means rising debt?▾

The case for

Laos was the most isolated country in Southeast Asia. Roads were terrible. There was no railway. Without Chinese money, Vientiane would still be a day's drive from anywhere important. The new railway, dams, and roads create jobs, slash transport costs, and finally let Lao products compete in regional markets. Refusing the deal would just keep Laos poor.

The case against

Debt that can't be repaid hands the lender enormous leverage. If Laos can't service its loans, what happens — does it lose control over key infrastructure, as critics fear has happened in Sri Lanka and Zambia? Some Lao quietly compare today's situation to the French colonial period: foreign capital making the big decisions about Lao land and labor.

Your turn: Is there a difference between accepting investment and accepting influence? Where would you draw the line?

⚡ 2. Is the "Battery of Southeast Asia" plan worth the cost?▾

The case for

Hydropower is low-carbon, sells for foreign currency, and uses a resource Laos has plenty of. It's how a poor mountain country becomes a middle-income one. Rich countries dammed their rivers a century ago — telling Laos not to is hypocritical.

The case against

The Mekong is shared. Dams in Laos hurt fishers in Cambodia, rice farmers in Vietnam, and fishing communities along the way. The 2018 Xepian-Xe Nam Noy collapse killed dozens. A short-term cash crop on the river may permanently harm one of the world's most productive freshwater ecosystems.

Your turn: Who should decide what happens to a river that crosses many countries? Is there a fair way to share the benefits and the costs?

🏛️ 3. Can a one-party state deliver what young Lao want?▾

The case for

The LPRP-led government has kept Laos politically stable, ended a long civil war, and gradually opened the economy. It has lifted millions out of extreme poverty and avoided the chaos that engulfed neighbors at various points. Stability has its own value, especially for a small, poor, landlocked country.

The case against

Young Lao are online, watch Thai and Korean media, talk to relatives abroad, and notice the gap between the official story and daily reality — the rising prices, the corruption, the limits on speaking your mind. The hundreds of thousands who leave each year for jobs in Thailand are a quiet vote about whether the current system serves them. International press-freedom and rights organizations consistently rank Laos low.

Your turn: Is "stability" enough on its own? What would you ask a government for — and how would you ask for it?

🛕 4. Does UNESCO save heritage, or freeze it?▾

The case for

Before UNESCO listing in 1995, Luang Prabang's old temples and colonial buildings were being torn down. The listing brought conservation rules, tourist money to maintain them, and global recognition that gave Lao people pride in their own heritage. Same for Vat Phou and the Plain of Jars.

The case against

UNESCO listing turns a living town into a museum — with rules made partly for outsiders. Locals who can't afford rising rents move out. Sacred rituals like the dawn alms-giving become photo opportunities. The "authentic" town is preserved on the outside; the community that made it is hollowed out.

Your turn: Is there a way to protect old places without turning them into stage sets? What would you do differently if you ran the UNESCO listing for Luang Prabang?

🇺🇸 5. What does the world owe Laos for the Secret War?▾

The facts

From 1964–1973, the U.S. dropped more bombs on Laos than were dropped in all of WWII. Around 30% didn't explode. Eighty million still lie in Lao soil. Children still die from them every year. The U.S. has funded clearance — but at a level critics say is small compared with the cost of the bombing itself.

The case for active reparations

The U.S. caused the contamination; it has a moral obligation to fund clearance until it's done — at whatever pace Laos requests, not whatever pace fits the U.S. budget. Other countries that bombed Laos, supplied weapons, or supported the war (including Thailand) share responsibility.

The complicated reality

Aid relationships come with conditions. Some Lao officials are cautious about how much foreign clearance work to accept, partly because of sovereignty concerns. Diaspora Hmong-Americans have a different stake again: the war scattered their families. There is no single "Lao" view on what owe-and-repay looks like.

Your turn: If a country bombs another country, when (if ever) does the debt end? What forms can repayment take — money, technology, an apology, citizenship for refugees?

📱 6. Should young Lao leave, or stay?▾

Going

A factory job in Bangkok pays several times what the same hours pay in Vientiane. Education abroad opens doors. Money sent home (remittances) keeps families afloat and pays for siblings' school fees. The world is bigger than the village.

Staying

If everyone with skills leaves, who builds the country's hospitals, schools, businesses? The "brain drain" is real, and a country with no doctors or engineers struggles to develop. Some Lao who studied abroad come back with new ideas and energy. Maybe the answer isn't either/or.

Your turn: If you were a 17-year-old in a Lao village with a chance to work in Thailand, what would you choose — and why? What would change your mind?

🎯 How to use these questions

These aren't trivia. They're real arguments that scholars, governments, and the Lao people themselves disagree about. A few ways to engage:

  • Pick one and write 300 words — make the strongest case you can for the side you disagree with first.
  • Debate with a friend — assign sides at random.
  • Find a Lao voice — listen to a podcast or read a piece by a Lao or Lao-diaspora writer (Kao Kalia Yang, Bryan Thao Worra, the Laotian Times) and notice how their take differs from textbook framing.
  • Update your mind in public — try answering the question, then read more, then write what changed. That's how thinking actually works.

These are open questions. Reasonable, well-informed people disagree. That's what makes them worth asking.

🖼️ Icons of Laos

Eight things you'll see again and again in Laos — drawn just for you. Each one tells a little story about the country.

The Lao Flag

ທຸງຊາດ · thung saat

Red bands for the people's courage, blue for the Mekong and prosperity, and a white circle for the full moon over the river — and for national unity.

Dok Champa

ດອກຈຳປາ · frangipani

The national flower. Its sweet white-and-yellow blooms stand for sincerity and joy, and are tucked behind the ear at festivals.

Pha That Luang

ພະທາດຫຼວງ

The great golden stupa in Vientiane, built in 1566. The most sacred monument in Laos — and the national symbol, right on the country's official seal.

The Wat (Temple)

ວັດ · wat

Every village has one. With swooping roofs and naga-serpent guardians on the stairs, the wat is the heart of worship, learning and community.

The Elephant

ຊ້າງ · saang

Laos was once "Lan Xang" — the Land of a Million Elephants. These gentle giants remain a proud symbol of the nation.

The Khaen

ແຄນ · khaen

A bamboo mouth-organ and the national instrument. Its breathy, buzzing music leads the lively mor lam folk-singing style.

Tip Khao

ຕິບເຂົ້າ · rice basket

The woven bamboo basket that keeps sticky rice warm. Lao people love it so much they call themselves "luk khao niaw" — children of the sticky rice!

Racing Boat

ເຮືອ · heua

Long, naga-prowed boats raced by whole villages on the rivers at the end of Buddhist Lent, cheered on by huge, joyful crowds.
